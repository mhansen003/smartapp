Research Methodology

Our stakeholder research followed a structured approach to capture business priorities, technical constraints, compliance requirements, and user needs across the organization. We conducted semi-structured interviews with key stakeholders from executive leadership, product management, engineering, compliance, operations, and business development teams.

Phase 1: Stakeholder Identification & Prioritization

We mapped the organizational structure and identified key decision-makers, subject matter experts, and individuals who would be most impacted by Smart App 2.0. Stakeholders were categorized by influence level and domain expertise to ensure comprehensive coverage of all project dimensions.

Phase 2: Interview Protocol Development

Developed customized interview guides for each stakeholder type, including questions about strategic goals, pain points with current systems, success metrics, budget constraints, regulatory concerns, technical infrastructure, and integration requirements. Questions were tailored based on each stakeholder's role and area of responsibility.

Phase 3: Conducted Semi-Structured Interviews

Sessions ranged from 60-120 minutes and were recorded with permission. We used a conversational approach to uncover both explicit requirements and implicit needs. Follow-up questions probed for specific examples, success criteria, and potential obstacles.

Phase 4: Synthesis & Theme Identification

Transcripts were analyzed to identify common themes, conflicting priorities, gaps in understanding, and areas requiring additional clarification. Key insights were documented with supporting quotes and categorized by domain (technical, business, compliance, UX, etc.).

Phase 5: Validation & Feedback Loop

Preliminary findings were shared with stakeholders for validation and refinement. This iterative process ensured accuracy and helped surface additional insights that emerged through reflection and discussion.

Strategic Vision & Business Goals

"We're not just building a digital mortgage app‚Äîwe're reimagining the entire borrower experience. The current market has low completion rates, around 40-50%, which means half of borrowers who start an application never finish. That's a massive opportunity loss. ‚Äî Paul Martinez, Chief Product Officer
Q: What are the top 3 business objectives for Smart App 2.0?
1. Increase application completion rate from 45% to 75%+ within first year
This is our north star metric. We need to reduce friction, provide real-time assistance, and make the process feel more collaborative rather than a cold transaction.

2. Reduce time-to-submit from average 4-7 days to under 24 hours
Borrowers hate the back-and-forth. They want speed and certainty. With Prove, Plaid, and TRUV integrations, we should be able to auto-populate 70-80% of required data instantly.

3. Differentiate in market with AI-powered assistance
Our competitors are still using basic form flows. We have the opportunity to integrate Claude AI as an always-available assistant that can answer questions, guide borrowers, and even help LOs prioritize their pipeline. This could be a game-changer for conversion rates.
Q: What does success look like in 6 months? 12 months? 24 months?
6 Months (MVP Launch):
- Beta launch with 10-15 top-performing LOs
- 60%+ completion rate (20% improvement over current)
- Prove integration live and working smoothly
- Positive NPS score (50+) from both borrowers and LOs

12 Months (Full Production):
- All LOs migrated to Smart App 2.0
- 75%+ completion rate
- Average time-to-submit under 36 hours
- Plaid and TRUV fully integrated
- AI Assistant answering 80%+ of borrower questions without human intervention
- 30% reduction in LO administrative time

24 Months (Market Leadership):
- Industry-leading completion rates (80%+)
- White-label solution launched for partner lenders
- Predictive analytics helping LOs close more deals
- Mobile-first experience with 50%+ of applications started on mobile
- Recognition as innovation leader in mortgage tech
Q: What are your biggest concerns or potential roadblocks?
Three main concerns keep me up at night:

1. Compliance complexity: Mortgage is one of the most regulated industries. Every feature we build has to go through compliance review. We need Kelli's team embedded from day one, not as a gatekeeper but as a partner in design.

2. LO adoption: Loan officers are creatures of habit. If Smart App 2.0 is perceived as "more work" or too different from what they know, they'll resist it. We need to make it so obviously better that they can't imagine going back.

3. Technical execution: We're integrating with multiple third-party services, our LOS, and building new features simultaneously. Scope creep is real. We need ruthless prioritization and a phased rollout plan.
Q: How do you envision the relationship between borrowers, LOs, and the system?
I see it as a collaborative triangle. The borrower should feel empowered and informed, not confused and dependent. The LO should be elevated to a strategic advisor, not a data entry clerk. And the system should be an intelligent assistant to both, not a bureaucratic obstacle.

Concretely, this means:
- Borrowers can self-serve most of the application but have easy access to their LO when needed
- LOs can see exactly where each borrower is stuck and intervene proactively

Technical Architecture & Infrastructure

"The current system is a monolith that's been patched and extended for 8 years. Every change is risky because everything is interconnected. Smart App 2.0 gives us the opportunity to build a modern, microservices-based architecture that's actually maintainable and scalable. But we need to be smart about it‚Äîdon't over-engineer, and don't try to solve every theoretical problem on day one."

‚Äî Yaz Chen, VP of Engineering
Q: What are the critical technical requirements for Smart App 2.0?
1. API-First Architecture: Everything should be built as APIs from day one. This enables us to support multiple front-ends (web, mobile app later, LO portal, partner integrations) without duplicating business logic.

2. Scalability: We need to handle 10x our current volume without major rework. That means async processing for heavy operations, proper caching strategy, and cloud infrastructure that can scale horizontally.

3. Real-time Updates: When a borrower uploads a document or answers questions, the LO should see it immediately. WebSockets or Server-Sent Events for real-time sync across clients.

4. Data Security: PII encryption at rest and in transit. Role-based access control. Audit logging for all data access. SOC 2 compliance is non-negotiable.

5. Resilience: Third-party services will fail. Prove, Plaid, TRUV‚Äîthey all have occasional outages. We need graceful degradation, retry logic, and fallback mechanisms so borrowers can still make progress even when an integration is down.
Q: What's your preferred tech stack and why?
Backend: Node.js with TypeScript. Our team knows it well, it's great for I/O-heavy operations (which we have with all these API integrations), and the ecosystem is mature. Express or Fastify for the API layer.

Frontend: React with Next.js. Server-side rendering for better SEO and initial load performance. TypeScript for type safety. Tailwind CSS for consistent styling without bloat.

Database: PostgreSQL for transactional data. It's rock-solid, handles complex queries well, and has great JSON support for flexible schemas. Redis for caching and session management.

Infrastructure: AWS. We're already there, team has expertise, and services like Lambda, ECS, RDS, and S3 cover everything we need. Terraform for infrastructure as code.

Monitoring: DataDog for application performance monitoring, Sentry for error tracking, CloudWatch for infrastructure metrics.
Q: What are the integration challenges with existing systems?
LOS Integration (Encompass): This is the biggest pain point. Encompass has an API but it's SOAP-based and not well documented. We'll need to build a robust abstraction layer. The data model is complex and we need to map Smart App data structures to Encompass fields carefully. Tom Bradley is our expert here‚Äîhe's integrated with Encompass before and knows the gotchas.

Third-Party Services: Prove, Plaid, TRUV all have modern REST APIs which is good. But each has different error handling, rate limiting, and webhook patterns. We need a unified integration framework so we're not reinventing the wheel for each service.

Data Consistency: Borrowers might be in multiple systems at once during transition. We need a clear data synchronization strategy and source of truth for each data element. Can't have conflicting information between Smart App and LOS.

Authentication: Current system uses old session-based auth. We should move to JWT tokens for APIs, but need to integrate with existing SSO for LOs. Dual authentication during transition period.
Q: What's your biggest technical concern?
Honestly? Over-engineering and gold-plating. I've seen too many projects fail because engineers wanted to build the "perfect" system. We need to ship an MVP that works, then iterate. Start with monolith-first approach (but well-structured), then extract microservices only when we have clear performance or team boundaries that require it.

Also concerned about technical debt from rushing. It's a balance‚Äîmove fast but don't cut corners on security, data integrity, or code quality. We need automated tests from day one, not "we'll add them later."

And the elephant in the room: maintaining both old and new systems during transition. We'll need a gradual migration strategy, which means extra complexity and cost. Budget for that.

Months 1-2 (Foundation):
- Architecture design and technical spec
- Set up dev/staging/prod environments
- Core API framework and authentication
- Database schema design
- CI/CD pipeline

Months 3-4 (Integrations & Core Features):

Months 5-6 (Polish & Beta):

- Beta testing with selected LOs

Months 7-8 (Production Rollout):
- Address beta feedback
- Performance optimization
- Training and documentation
- Gradual rollout to all LOs
- 24/7 monitoring and support

Regulatory Requirements & Compliance Framework

"Innovation is great, but in mortgage, compliance isn't optional. Every disclosure, every data field, every communication with the borrower is governed by federal and state regulations. We can't launch Smart App 2.0 if it doesn't meet RESPA, TILA, ECOA, and fair lending requirements. The good news is that digital systems can actually improve compliance if designed correctly‚Äîbetter audit trails, consistent processes, fewer human errors."

‚Äî Kelli Thompson, Chief Compliance Officer
Q: What are the critical compliance requirements we must address?
1. TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosures (TRID):
Loan Estimate must be provided within 3 business days of application. Application is defined as name, income, SSN, property address, estimate of property value, and loan amount. We need to capture these six pieces of information and trigger disclosure generation automatically. Timing is critical‚Äîany changes to key fields may require new disclosures with 3-day waiting periods.

2. Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA):
We cannot discriminate based on protected classes. This means careful review of any AI/ML features‚Äîif the AI is making recommendations or auto-filling data, we need to ensure it's not introducing bias. Also, GMI (Government Monitoring Information) collection is required and must follow specific formats and opt-out language.

3. Fair Lending & HMDA Reporting:
Every application must be tracked for HMDA reporting requirements. We need to collect demographic data (race, ethnicity, sex) with proper disclosure language. Data must be reportable even for applications that don't proceed to closing.

4. Data Privacy & Security:
GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act) requires privacy notices and opt-out provisions. All PII must be protected. We're also subject to state privacy laws (CCPA in California, etc.). Right to deletion, right to access, consent management‚Äîall need to be built into the system.

5. E-Sign Compliance:
Electronic signatures and consents require specific disclosures. Borrowers must affirmatively consent to electronic delivery. We need audit trails showing who signed what and when. Can't just assume they agree because they used the system.

6. State-Specific Requirements:
Every state has additional rules. Some require specific disclosures, some have cooling-off periods, some restrict certain fees. System needs to be configurable by state.
Q: What approval process do we need for new features?
Every user-facing feature needs compliance review before development starts, not after. Here's the process:

1. Requirements Review (Before Development):
Product provides mockups/wireframes and user flows. Compliance reviews for regulatory requirements, required disclosures, consent language, and fair lending implications. We provide feedback within 5 business days.

2. UAT Review (Before Beta):
We test the actual feature in staging environment. Verify disclosures are correct, timing is appropriate, data is captured correctly, audit trails are working. Sign-off required before beta testing.

3. Production Review (Before General Release):
Final check after beta feedback is incorporated. Confirm no compliance-related changes were made without review. Final sign-off for production release.

Critical:** Any AI features get extra scrutiny. We need to understand the logic, ensure it's not discriminatory, and have clear override capabilities for edge cases.
Q: What are your concerns about third-party integrations like Prove, Plaid, and TRUV?
Third-party vendors are our responsibility from a compliance perspective. If Prove makes a mistake in identity verification, we're liable. If Plaid exposes borrower data, we're accountable.

Due Diligence Requirements:
- Full vendor risk assessment for each provider
- Review their security practices and certifications
- Ensure they're FCRA compliant (especially for credit-related data)
- Verify they have E&O insurance
- Contracts must include compliance provisions and SLAs

Ongoing Monitoring:
- Quarterly review of vendor performance
- Track any incidents or data breaches
- Annual re-certification

Specific Concerns:
- Prove: How do we verify the person on the phone is actually the applicant? What if someone's phone is stolen? Need fallback verification methods.
- Plaid: Borrower consents to Plaid accessing their bank accounts. We need explicit consent language and ability to revoke access. Also, what if Plaid data conflicts with what borrower tells us?
- TRUV: Employment and income data is sensitive. Need clear audit trail of what was retrieved and when. Borrowers should be able to review and correct if inaccurate.
Q: How do we handle the AI assistant from a compliance perspective?
This is new territory for mortgage compliance, so we need to be extra careful.

Key Requirements:
1. Disclosure: Borrowers must know they're interacting with AI, not a human. Clear labeling in the interface.

2. Accuracy: AI cannot provide inaccurate information about loan terms, rates, or requirements. Everything it says must be auditable and correct. If it makes a mistake, we're liable.

3. Fairness: AI must not treat protected classes differently. We'll need to monitor interactions for bias patterns. Regular audits of AI responses.

4. Human Oversight: There must always be a way for borrower to reach a human LO. AI is assistance, not replacement.

5. Data Usage: What the AI learns from borrower interactions is subject to privacy rules. Can't use one borrower's data to train responses for others without consent.

6. Audit Trail: Every AI interaction logged. If there's ever a complaint or lawsuit, we need to show exactly what the AI told the borrower.

My recommendation: Start conservative. Limit AI to factual Q&A and process guidance. No rate quotes, no loan recommendations, no qualification decisions. Expand scope gradually as we build confidence.
Q: What documentation and audit trails are required?
Comprehensive audit logging is non-negotiable. We need to be able to reconstruct every step of every application for regulatory exams and potential litigation.

Must Log:
- All data entered (what, when, by whom)
- All data changes (before/after values, timestamp, user)
- All documents uploaded/downloaded
- All communications (emails, SMS, AI chat logs)
- All third-party API calls and responses
- All disclosures delivered (what, when, method, receipt confirmation)
- All consents obtained (e-sign, privacy, credit pull, etc.)
- All access to borrower data (who, when, what they viewed)

Retention: Minimum 5 years for closed loans, 3 years for incomplete applications. Some states require longer. California is 7 years. Recommend 7 years across the board to be safe.

Format: Logs must be tamper-evident. Write-once storage. Searchable and exportable for audits. No one (including admins) should be able to delete or modify historical logs.

Operational Requirements & Change Management

"The success of Smart App 2.0 isn't just about building great technology‚Äîit's about adoption. We have 200+ loan officers with varying levels of tech savviness. Some are digital natives, others still print every email. If we don't nail the training, support, and change management, even the best system will fail."

‚Äî Marcus Johnson, Director of Operations
Q: What are the operational challenges with the current system?
Fragmented Workflow: LOs jump between 5-6 different systems daily. Encompass for loan data, a separate portal for document requests, email for borrower communication, spreadsheets for pipeline management. It's inefficient and error-prone.

Inconsistent Processes: Every LO has their own way of doing things. Some send initial disclosures within hours, others take 2-3 days. Some call borrowers proactively, others wait for borrowers to reach out. This inconsistency hurts our brand and makes training new LOs difficult.

Support Burden: Our support team gets hammered with basic questions. "How do I reset my password?" "Where did the borrower's documents go?" "Why hasn't the credit report pulled?" These are system issues that technology should solve.

Limited Visibility: Management has no real-time view into what's happening. We pull reports weekly, but by then opportunities are missed. We need live dashboards showing where applications are stuck, which LOs need help, where bottlenecks exist.
Q: What does the ideal LO workflow look like in Smart App 2.0?
Morning Routine (10 minutes):
- Log into Smart App dashboard
- See prioritized list of "hot" applications (borrower engaged, LO action needed)
- Review any borrower questions from overnight (AI answered most, flagged ones requiring LO)
- Send any needed follow-ups

During the Day:
- Get real-time notifications when borrower completes sections or uploads docs
- One-click review and approve/reject documents
- Inline communication with borrower (no switching to email)
- See exactly what's missing with automated checklists

End of Day (5 minutes):
- Quick review of pipeline status
- Send any scheduled reminders to borrowers
- Flag any applications at risk of going stale

The key is that the system does the heavy lifting. LOs focus on high-value activities: relationship building, answering complex questions, closing deals. Not data entry and status checking.
Q: What training and change management will be required?
This is a major change, so we need a comprehensive plan:

Pre-Launch (2 months before):
- Town hall meetings introducing Smart App 2.0 and the "why"
- Champion identification‚Äîfind enthusiastic early adopters in each region
- Create excitement, not fear

Training Program:
- Role-based training: LO training is different from LO manager training
- Multiple formats: live webinars, recorded videos, written guides, practice environment
- Hands-on practice with test scenarios before production launch
- Certification program‚ÄîLOs must complete training before getting access

Beta Phase (1-2 months):
- Select 10-15 champion LOs to use system first
- Weekly feedback sessions
- Rapid iteration on pain points
- Document FAQs and best practices from beta

Rollout (Phased over 2-3 months):
- Start with top performers who embrace technology
- Gradual expansion to broader population
- Mandatory training sessions for each cohort
- Super-users in each region who can provide peer support

Ongoing Support:
- Dedicated Slack/Teams channel for questions
- Weekly office hours with product team
- Monthly "tips and tricks" training
- Continuous improvement based on feedback
Q: How do we measure operational success?
We need clear, measurable KPIs:

Efficiency Metrics:
- Time to send initial disclosures (target: <4 hours)
- Time from application start to completion (target: <24 hours)
- LO time spent per application (target: 30% reduction)
- Number of systems LOs need to access (target: down to 2)

Quality Metrics:
- Application completion rate (target: 75%+)
- Data accuracy (fewer corrections needed downstream)
- Borrower satisfaction (NPS 50+)
- LO satisfaction (NPS 40+)

Adoption Metrics:
- % of LOs actively using Smart App vs. old system
- % of new applications started in Smart App
- Feature utilization (are LOs using AI assistant, document review, etc.)
- Support ticket volume (should decrease over time)

Business Impact:
- Pull-through rate (applications that close)
- Average loan amount
- Cost per funded loan
- LO productivity (funded loans per LO per month)
Q: What support infrastructure do we need?
Tier 1 Support (Help Desk):
- Handle basic questions, password resets, access issues
- Available 7am-7pm ET (when LOs are active)
- Target response time: <15 minutes
- Self-service knowledge base for common issues

Tier 2 Support (Product Specialists):
- Handle complex workflow questions, data issues, integration problems
- Available during business hours
- Target response time: <2 hours
- Escalation path to engineering for bugs

Tier 3 Support (Engineering):
- Critical system issues, outages, data corruption
- On-call rotation 24/7
- Target response time: <30 minutes for critical issues

Proactive Monitoring:
- System health dashboards
- Automated alerts for anomalies (spike in errors, integration failures, etc.)
- Weekly review of trends and improvement opportunities

Lisa Patel will lead the training program. We need at least 2 FTEs dedicated to Smart App 2.0 support initially, then reassess after 6 months.

Yaz (Engineering): "6 months is aggressive. We can't compromise on quality and security."
Resolution: Phased approach with clear MVP definition. Launch beta in 6 months with core features (Prove integration, basic application flow, LO dashboard). Add Plaid, TRUV, and AI assistant in subsequent releases over following 3-4 months. Prioritize ruthlessly‚Äîfeatures that don't support completion rate improvement get deferred.
Innovation vs. Compliance Risk
Paul (Product): "AI assistant is our differentiator. It needs to be front and center."
Kelli (Compliance): "AI in mortgage is uncharted territory. We need to be conservative until we understand the risks."
Resolution: Launch AI assistant with limited scope initially (FAQs, process guidance, definitions). No rate quotes, no qualification decisions, no loan recommendations in v1. Expand capabilities gradually with compliance approval after monitoring first 1000 interactions for bias and accuracy issues. Clear "Talk to Human LO" option always visible.
Best-in-Class UX vs. Development Timeline
Sarah (Product) & Amanda (UX): "Borrowers expect Netflix-level polish. We need animations, micro-interactions, delightful details."
Yaz (Engineering): "Polish takes time. We can build functional first, beautify later."
Resolution: Define "polish" as clear hierarchy, readable typography, logical flow, and helpful error messages‚Äînot fancy animations. Focus on usability fundamentals in MVP. Add delightful micro-interactions in v1.1 after core functionality is proven. Use design system (Tailwind UI or similar) to get professional look without custom development.
LO Control vs. Automation
LOs (via Marcus): "We need control over borrower communication and process. Don't auto-send things without our approval."
Paul (Product): "Automation is how we achieve speed. Too much manual LO involvement defeats the purpose."
Resolution: Configuration options at LO level (per Marcus and LO Config requirements). LOs can choose their automation level: Full Auto (system sends disclosures, reminders, etc. automatically), Semi-Auto (system prepares but LO approves before sending), Manual (LO initiates everything). Default to Semi-Auto, let LOs opt into Full Auto as they gain confidence.
Custom Features vs. Standardization
Jennifer (BD): "Partner lenders want customization. Different branding, different workflows, different integrations."
Yaz (Engineering): "Customization doesn't scale. Every custom feature is technical debt."
Resolution: Build configurability, not customization. White-label theming (colors, logos, fonts) via config file. Feature toggles for optional modules. Standard API for partner integrations. Custom development only for strategic partnerships above certain revenue threshold. 80% of partners get standard product, 20% get custom features if they pay for it.
Budget Constraints vs. Third-Party Costs
Elena (CFO): "Prove, Plaid, and TRUV have per-transaction costs. At scale, these could be significant. We need to manage costs."
Paul (Product): "These integrations are core to the value proposition. We can't compromise here."
Resolution: Negotiate volume-based pricing with vendors. Build in usage analytics to track ROI per integration. Consider offering "lite" version without premium integrations for price-sensitive borrowers. Make Plaid and TRUV optional (borrowers can manually upload documents instead) but default to integrations for best experience. Monitor cost-per-funded-loan closely and adjust pricing strategy if needed.
